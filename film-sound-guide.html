I. General Sound and Film Concepts

Added Value ▼
The informative or expressive enhancement that sound brings to the image in film. This concept is useful for analysing how sound enriches our understanding of what we see.
Example: In 'The Godfather', the sound of a train during Michael's murder of Sollozzo and McCluskey adds dramatic tension without any train being visible.
Audiovisual Contract ▼
The unconscious process by which viewers connect what they see and hear in a film, creating a unified experience. This concept helps explain how we make sense of film as an audiovisual medium.
Example: When a door closes in a film, we expect to hear a corresponding sound, fulfilling our 'contract' with the film's reality.
Image Track ▼
The visual component of a film seen on screen. It works together with the sound track to create the overall film experience.
Example: In silent movies, the image track carried the entire narrative weight.
Narrative Clarity ▼
The principle of presenting a film's story as clearly as possible, even if it means sacrificing some realism. This is important for understanding how filmmakers prioritise storytelling.
Example: Dialogue in crowd scenes is often unnaturally clear to ensure viewers can follow the narrative.
Plot ▼
The order in which events are presented in the film, which may differ from the chronological story. Studying the plot reveals how filmmakers create suspense or surprise.
Example: Christopher Nolan's 'Memento' has a plot that moves backward while the story actually moves forward chronologically.
Sound Track ▼
All the audible elements in a film, including dialogue, music, and sound effects. It plays a crucial role in storytelling and creating atmosphere.
Example: The sound track of 'Apocalypse Now' creates an immersive jungle atmosphere through layered ambient sounds.
Story ▼
The events of a film in chronological order. Analysing the story helps understand the basic narrative structure.
Example: The story of 'The Godfather' spans approximately ten years in the life of the Corleone family.
Synchronisation ▼
The precise alignment of sound and image in film. Good synchronisation helps create a believable film world.
Example: The sync of lip movements with dialogue or footsteps with walking characters maintains the illusion of reality.

II. Sound Categories and Sources

Acousmatic Sound ▼
Sounds heard in a film without seeing their source on screen. This technique can create mystery or tension.
Example: The unseen antagonist in 'The Blair Witch Project' is represented only through acousmatic sounds in the forest.
Ambient Sound ▼
Background noises that establish the setting of a scene, like city traffic or birds chirping. These sounds help create a sense of place and atmosphere.
Example: The ambient sounds of cricket chirps and distant thunder in 'Days of Heaven' establish the rural Texas setting.
Diegetic Sound ▼
Sounds that originate from within the world of the film, such as character dialogue or music playing on a radio in the scene. These sounds help create a realistic film world.
Example: In 'Pulp Fiction', when Mia and Vincent dance at Jack Rabbit Slim's, the music is diegetic as it's playing on the restaurant's jukebox.
Foley ▼
Recreated everyday sound effects added to a film in post-production, such as footsteps or door creaks. Foley adds realism and texture to the sound track.
Example: The lightsaber sounds in 'Star Wars' were created by Foley artists combining various real-world sounds.
Non-diegetic Sound ▼
Sounds added to the film that characters can't hear, like background music or narration. These sounds often guide the viewer's emotional response.
Example: The ominous theme in 'Jaws' that plays when the shark approaches is non-diegetic – only the audience hears it.

III. Sound Characteristics and Techniques

Consonance ▼
When musical notes played together sound pleasant and stable. Consonance often creates a sense of resolution or peace.
Example: The final chord in 'The Shawshank Redemption' score when Andy and Red reunite on the beach is consonant, signifying closure.
Dissonance ▼
When musical notes played together sound tense or unstable. Dissonance can create tension or unease in a scene.
Example: The screeching violin notes in 'Psycho's' shower scene use dissonance to heighten the horror.
Harmony ▼
The combination of different musical notes played simultaneously. Harmony can evoke specific emotions and enhance the mood of a scene.
Example: The rich, warm harmonies in 'Cinema Paradiso's' score by Ennio Morricone evoke nostalgia.
Meter ▼
The regular pulse or beat in music, often grouped into measures. Meter provides structure and can influence how we perceive time in a film.
Example: The steady 3/4 waltz meter in '2001: A Space Odyssey' during the space station docking sequence emphasizes the graceful rotation.
Orchestration ▼
The art of choosing and arranging instruments in a piece of music. Effective orchestration can enhance the emotional impact of film music.
Example: John Williams' orchestration for 'Star Wars' uses full brass for the heroic main theme, helping establish its epic nature.
Pitch ▼
The highness or lowness of a sound. Pitch can convey emotion and create tension or release.
Example: The gradually rising pitch of the strings in 'Jaws' as the shark approaches creates mounting tension.
Rhythm ▼
The pattern of sounds and silences in music or other sounds. Rhythm can create mood and help pace the narrative.
Example: The rhythmic typing sounds in 'The Social Network' underscore Mark Zuckerberg's coding scenes, emphasizing his intense focus.
Tempo ▼
The speed or pace of music or rhythmic sounds in a film. Tempo can affect the perceived pacing of a scene.
Example: The increasing tempo during chase scenes in 'Mad Max: Fury Road' heightens the sense of urgency and danger.
Tessitura ▼
The general pitch range of a piece of music or a voice. Tessitura can affect the overall mood and energy of a musical piece or vocal performance.
Example: The low tessitura of Hans Zimmer's 'Inception' score adds weight and seriousness to the film.
Timbre ▼
The unique quality or 'colour' of a sound that distinguishes it from others, even at the same pitch and volume. Timbre helps create rich, varied soundscapes.
Example: Darth Vader's breathing has a distinctive timbre that immediately identifies his character, even off-screen.
Volume/Dynamics ▼
The loudness or softness of a sound. Changes in volume can create emphasis or suggest distance.
Example: In 'No Country for Old Men', the subtle volume changes of Anton Chigurh's footsteps create tension through their barely audible presence.

IV. Sound Track Textures

A-melodic ▼
A texture of background sounds or music without a clear melody. This can create atmosphere without drawing attention away from dialogue or action.
Example: The ambient electronic textures in 'Blade Runner 2049' create mood without melodic elements that might distract from the dialogue.
Homophony (music) ▼
A texture with a main melody supported by chords moving in the same rhythm. This is common in many film scores.
Example: John Williams' 'Imperial March' from 'Star Wars' features a clear melody with chords underneath that follow the same rhythmic pattern.
Melody and accompaniment (music) ▼
A texture with a clear main tune supported by a more complex background. This allows for both melodic expression and harmonic richness.
Example: The main theme from 'Titanic' by James Horner features a prominent melody with a richer, more complex accompaniment beneath.
Monophony ▼
A texture with only one main sound or melody, without accompaniment. This can create focus or simplicity in a scene.
Example: The lone flute melody that opens 'Once Upon a Time in America' creates an intimate, solitary mood.
Polyphony ▼
A texture with multiple, equally important melodies or sounds occurring simultaneously. This can create complexity or represent multiple perspectives.
Example: The overlapping dialogue and sound effects in Robert Altman's films like 'Nashville' create a polyphonic soundscape that represents multiple character perspectives.

V. Sound Design and Production

Density ▼
The perceived fullness or complexity of the mix of sounds in a scene. Density can affect the mood and energy of a scene.
Example: The opening battle scene in 'Saving Private Ryan' uses high sound density with overlapping explosions, gunfire, and shouting to create overwhelming chaos.
Layering ▼
The technique of combining multiple sounds to create a richer, more complex audio experience. Layering adds depth to the sound track.
Example: In 'Jurassic Park', the T-Rex roar was created by layering tiger, alligator, and elephant sounds to create something both believable and otherworldly.
Liveliness ▼
The amount of activity or movement in the sounds of a scene. Liveliness can influence the perceived pace and energy of a scene.
Example: The bustling market scenes in 'Slumdog Millionaire' use highly lively sound design with constant movement and activity to convey the energy of Mumbai.
Sound Design ▼
The process of planning, creating, and arranging all the sounds for a film. Good sound design enhances storytelling and viewer engagement.
Example: Walter Murch's sound design for 'Apocalypse Now' creates a rich sonic environment that immerses viewers in the psychological experience of war.
Sound Mixing ▼
The process of balancing and adjusting different audio elements to create the final sound track. Mixing is crucial for clarity and emotional impact.
Example: In 'Whiplash', the precise mixing of the jazz performances allows individual instruments to stand out at key moments while maintaining the overall band sound.
Sound Perspective ▼
The apparent distance and direction of sounds relative to the camera or listener. This helps create a sense of space in the film world.
Example: In 'Gravity', sound perspective changes dramatically as the camera moves from inside to outside of a space helmet, simulating what the character would actually hear.
Sweetening ▼
The process of enhancing sound effects by mixing in additional sounds. This technique creates more impactful and realistic sound effects.
Example: In action films like 'John Wick', gunshots are sweetened with additional bass elements to make them sound more powerful than real gunshots.

VI. Music in Film

Anempathetic Music ▼
Music that seems indifferent to or contradicts the emotions in a scene. This can create irony or emotional distance.
Example: In 'Reservoir Dogs', the upbeat "Stuck in the Middle with You" plays during a violent torture scene, creating a disturbing contrast.
Empathetic Music ▼
Music that directly expresses or reinforces the emotions in a scene. This type of music intensifies the viewer's emotional experience.
Example: The sad piano theme in 'Schindler's List' directly reinforces and heightens the emotional impact of the tragic scenes.
Leitmotif ▼
A recurring musical theme associated with a particular character, place, or idea in a film. Leitmotifs help create coherence and depth in storytelling.
Example: The 'Raiders March' serves as Indiana Jones's leitmotif, appearing whenever he performs heroic actions across the series.
Pedal Point ▼
A sustained note, usually in the bass, that remains constant while other musical elements change above it. Pedal points can create tension or a sense of grounding.
Example: Hans Zimmer's score for 'Dunkirk' uses a continuous low pedal point throughout much of the film to create sustained tension.
Underscoring ▼
Background music in a film that supports the mood or action. Underscoring can guide viewers' emotions and enhance narrative understanding.
Example: In 'The Shawshank Redemption', Thomas Newman's gentle piano underscoring adds emotional depth to Andy and Red's friendship without drawing attention to itself.

VII. Narrative and Structural Elements

Audio Dissolve ▼
A transition technique often used in musicals, where diegetic sound (like a character singing) smoothly blends into non-diegetic sound (like orchestral accompaniment). This can create a dreamlike quality or signify a shift in the reality of the scene.
Example: In 'La La Land', when Sebastian plays piano at the restaurant, the solo piano (diegetic) dissolves into a full orchestral arrangement (non-diegetic) as the scene transitions.
Background ▼
Less prominent sounds that create the overall acoustic environment of a scene. Background sounds add depth and realism.
Example: In 'Casablanca', the ambient sounds of glasses clinking and distant conversations in Rick's Café create a believable background atmosphere.
Establishing Sound ▼
Sounds used at the beginning of a scene to establish the setting or atmosphere. These help orient the viewer in the film's world.
Example: The sound of seagulls and waves in the opening of 'The Truman Show' immediately establishes the coastal setting.
Foreground ▼
The main or most prominent sounds in a scene. These sounds typically carry the most narrative importance.
Example: In a dialogue scene from 'The Social Network', the characters' voices are in the foreground while the party sounds are pushed to the background.
Hard Cut ▼
An abrupt transition between scenes where both the image and sound change suddenly. This can create a jarring effect, often used to surprise the audience or emphasise a dramatic change in the story.
Example: In '2001: A Space Odyssey', the famous match cut from the bone to the spacecraft is accompanied by a hard cut from primitive screams to silence.
Mickey-Mousing ▼
A technique where music closely follows the action on screen, often imitating specific movements or events. This can create a comedic effect or emphasise particular actions.
Example: In 'The Pink Panther', the music follows Inspector Clouseau's clumsy movements, with musical accents for each stumble or fall.
Neutral Music ▼
Background music that doesn't actively reinforce or contradict the emotions of a scene. This type of music can create atmosphere without drawing attention to itself.
Example: In many documentary films, neutral background music provides pace and continuity without emotionally manipulating the audience's response to the content.
Sound Advance ▼
When audio from an upcoming scene is heard before the visual transition occurs. This technique can create anticipation or smooth scene changes.
Example: In 'The Godfather', we hear the train whistle before the scene transitions to the train station where Michael arrives in Sicily.
Sound Bridge ▼
The use of sound to connect two scenes, often by starting the audio of the next scene before the visual transition. This creates smoother scene transitions.
Example: In 'The Graduate', Simon and Garfunkel's music often bridges scenes, connecting different moments in Benjamin's life.
Sound Lag ▼
When audio from one scene continues briefly into the next scene. This technique can create connections between scenes or evoke a sense of memory.
Example: In 'Inception', Edith Piaf's "Non, Je Ne Regrette Rien" continues to play as characters transition between dream levels, acting as both a story element and sound lag.
Sound Link ▼
The use of continuous sound (like music or ambient noise) to connect a series of shots that might otherwise seem unrelated. This helps create a sense of unity.
Example: In 'Amélie', the narrator's voice serves as a sound link connecting various vignettes about different characters.
Sound Match ▼
When a sound at the end of one scene is followed by a similar sound at the beginning of the next. This creates a smooth transition between scenes.
Example: In 'Citizen Kane', the sound of Kane's wife singing operatically matches to the sound of a parrot squawking in the following scene, creating an ironic transition.
Stinger ▼
A sudden, sharp sound used for dramatic effect, often to startle the audience or emphasise a moment. Stingers can create tension or punctuate dramatic moments.
Example: In 'Psycho', the sharp violin stabs during the shower scene serve as stingers that emphasize each knife strike.
Sync Point ▼
A moment when a specific sound perfectly matches a visual event on screen. Sync points can create satisfying audiovisual harmony or emphasise specific actions.
Example: In 'Baby Driver', the gunshots and car stunts are precisely synchronized with beats in the music, creating stylized sync points throughout the film.

VIII. Analysis Techniques

Commutation Test ▼
Replacing the original music in a scene with different music to understand the impact of the original choice. This test reveals how music shapes our interpretation of a scene.
Example: Replacing the 'Jaws' theme with cheerful music in shark approach scenes would demonstrate how crucial the original music is to creating tension.
Masking ▼
The practice of watching a film without sound or listening to the sound without images. This technique helps analyse how each component contributes to the overall effect.
Example: Watching the opening scene of 'Apocalypse Now' without sound reveals how much of the scene's impact depends on the Doors' "The End" and the helicopter sounds.
Vertical Montage ▼
The relationship between what is seen and heard at any given moment in a film. Analysing vertical montage helps understand how sound and image work together.
Example: In the baptism scene of 'The Godfather', the vertical montage of religious ceremony visuals with the sounds of gunshots and screams creates powerful juxtaposition.

IX. Specific Sound Effects

Crescendo ▼
A gradual increase in volume. Crescendos can build tension or excitement.
Example: In 'Close Encounters of the Third Kind', the music builds in a crescendo as the alien mothership appears, heightening the sense of awe.
Diminuendo ▼
A gradual decrease in volume. Diminuendos can create a sense of fading or calming.
Example: At the end of 'The Truman Show', the volume of the soundtrack gradually diminishes as Truman walks through the exit door, symbolizing his departure from the artificial world.
Fade In/Fade Out ▼
The gradual appearance or disappearance of a sound. These techniques can smooth transitions or suggest the passing of time.
Example: In 'Fight Club', the sounds of the support group meetings often fade out as the narrator loses focus, mirroring his mental state.
Reverb ▼
An effect that simulates sound reflections, creating a sense of space. Reverb can suggest the size and nature of an environment.
Example: In 'The King's Speech', the large reverb on the king's voice during his radio broadcasts emphasizes the vastness of his audience and the pressure he feels.

X. Film Techniques Related to Sound

Audiovisual Dissonance ▼
When the audio and visual elements of a scene seem to contradict each other. This can create unease or draw attention to the constructed nature of the film.
Example: In 'A Clockwork Orange', classical music plays during violent scenes, creating audiovisual dissonance that highlights the protagonist's disturbed character.
Establishing Shot ▼
A wide camera shot at the beginning of a scene that shows the overall setting. Often accompanied by establishing sounds to create a sense of place.
Example: The establishing shot of New York City in 'The Avengers' is accompanied by the sounds of car horns and city ambience to establish the urban environment.
Point-of-Audition ▼
The perspective from which sounds are heard in a film, often aligned with a character's position. This technique can increase viewer identification with characters.
Example: In 'Saving Private Ryan', we hear the muffled underwater sounds from Captain Miller's point-of-audition after the beach landing explosion, putting us in his perspective.
Shot/Reverse Shot ▼
A common technique for filming conversation, alternating between views of each speaker. The associated audio helps maintain the illusion of continuous dialogue.
Example: The interrogation scenes in 'The Dark Knight' use shot/reverse shot between the Joker and Batman while maintaining consistent audio to create a seamless conversation.
